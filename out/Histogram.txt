<p>A histogram is similar to a <a href="./Bar Graph">bar graph</a> but instead displays the frequency of <a href="./Interval">intervals</a> of <a href="./Variable (Statistics)#Quantitative">quantitative variables</a>.</p>
<h1>Instructions</h1>
<ol>
<li>Split the values of the variable into a set of continuous <a href="./Interval">intervals</a> of typically equal size.</li>
<li>For each <a href="./Interval">interval</a> count the total quantity of occurrences that fit into that <a href="./Interval">interval</a>.</li>
<li>Draw bars for each interval whose height is the quantity of occurrences counted before.</li>
</ol>
<h1>Properties</h1>
<h2>&quot;Modality&quot;</h2>
<p>The &quot;modality&quot; of a histogram describe where the peaks occur and are described in 3 main categories:</p>
<ul>
<li>Unimodal - One clear peak</li>
<li>Bimodal - Two clear peaks</li>
<li>Multimodal - More than 2 clear peaks</li>
</ul>

<li>Left Skewed - Most of the &quot;mass&quot; is on the right side and then a long smaller left trail</li>

<p>Numerically when a histogram is &quot;left skewed&quot;, it's <a href="./Median">median</a> will be much greater than it's <a href="./Mean">mean</a> and conversely if it's &quot;right skewed&quot;, it's <a href="./Median">median</a> will be a lot smaller. <h2>Spread</h2>
<p>The spread of a histogram describes how far most points usually are from the centre of the histogram.</p>
<h2>Outlier</h2>
<p>An outlier is a single observation in a histogram that is visibly removed from the main &quot;mass&quot; of observations, in other words it's unusually far from the centre of the histogram.</p>
