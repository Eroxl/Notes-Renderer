<h5>Pennsylvania</h5>
<h6>Following a Collusive Suit Brought by the League of Women Voters against the Democrat Secretary of the Commonwealth Seeking to Require that Absentee Ballots not Passing the Signature Verification Process Be given Notice and an Opportunity to Cure, the Secretary Unilaterally Abolished the Signature Verification Process Altogether, Issuing a Directive that not only Was it not Required, it Was not even Permitted. She then Filed an Emergency Writ Action with the Partisan-Elected Supreme Court to Ratify Her Elimination of that Statutory Requirement. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court Agreed with the Secretary, but Went Further, also Eliminating the Statutory Right of Candidates to Challenge Illegal Ballots during the Absentee Ballot Canvassing</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Pennsylvania officials colluded with the League of Women Voters suit to eliminate absentee ballot signature checks, then got the Philadelphia Supreme Court to ratify it.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>The League of Women Voters' (LWV) federal lawsuit (<a href="./League of Women Voters v. Boockvar">League of Women Voters v. Boockvar</a>) sought notice and cure for signature issues. <a href="./Secretary of State">Secretary</a> <a href="./Kathy Boockvar">Kathy Boockvar</a> responded on September 11, 2020, with guidance that counties shall not set aside ballots solely for signature mismatches.</p>
<p>This advisory was technically legal under Philadelphia election law (the Secretary interpreted that boards lacked authority to reject by signature alone). The LWV plaintiffs voluntarily dismissed the case the same day, and the court closed it on October 16, 2020. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-8"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-8').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [8]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>The litigation ended when the guidance made the claim moot. <a href="./Kathy Boockvar">Boockvar</a> then took the issue to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court in <a href="./In Re Nov 3, 2020 General Election"> In Re: Nov 3, 2020 General Election</a>. The Court agreed that state law did not allow rejecting ballots for signature mismatches. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-9"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-9').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [9]
          </a>
        </sup> Justice <a href="./Kevin Dougherty">Kevin Dougherty</a> wrote: &quot;the Election Code does not authorize or require local boards to reject&quot; ballots based solely on signature comparison. The ruling was unanimous. Thus, the Court effectively upheld the Secretary's guidance. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-9"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-9').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [9]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p>It is notable though that the Pennsylvania supreme court is made up of a <a href="Democratic%20Party">Democratic</a> majority. However the majority opinion was accepted 6–1 with one of which Chief Justice <a href="./Thomas G. Saylor">Thomas G. Saylor</a> being a <a href="./Republican Party">Republican</a> and despite not accepting the majority opinion Justice <a href="./Sallie Mundy">Sallie Mundy</a> the other <a href="./Republican Party">Republican</a> also concurred with the outcome (for <a href="./Kathy Boockvar">Boockvar</a>) just not the majority opinion.</p>
<p>The collusion that <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> alleged in the claim was unproven, and he failed to provide any evidence of such. Additionally, the rejection rate of mail in ballots between the 2016 election and the <a href="./2020 Election">2020 election</a> increased by +0.3% despite the relaxed signature verification rules.</p>

Boockvar</a>, the Trump campaign asked to allow more campaign observers in canvassing sites. The Commonwealth Court had ordered expanded access, but the Philadelphia Supreme Court (<a href="./Republican Party">Republicans</a> dissenting) vacated that order and reinstated the trial court's one-person-per-candidate rule. <sup class="citation">

          >
            [10]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>The Supreme Court held (5–2) (with the <a href="./Republican Party">Republican</a> judges dissenting) that the trial judge had correctly required only one representative per party inside the canvass site. The majority opinion affirmed that existing statute and regulation were satisfied by the election officials' set-up. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-10"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-10').scrollIntoView(); return false;"

<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that the Philadelphia Supreme Court eliminated requirements for proper completion of absentee ballots.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>On November 23, 2020, in <a href="./Trump v. Boockvar">Trump v. Boockvar</a>, the Court ruled that missing date/address on a ballot certificate does not void the vote. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-11"

This was a 5–2 decision with <a href="./Republican Party">Republican</a> judges dissenting. The Court allowed counting of ballots even if voters failed to hand-print the envelope date or address.</p>
<h5>Wisconsin</h5>
<h6>The Use of Unmanned Drop Boxes, not Authorized in Wisconsin Law</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Wisconsin's use of unmanned ballot drop boxes violated state law.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>Initially, in 2020 the state permitted absentee drop boxes (one per municipality). However, a later court challenge after the election found technical violations. In <a href="./Teigen v. Wisconsin Elections Commission">Teigen v. Wisconsin Elections Commission</a>, the conservative majority held that Wisconsin law did not authorize any drop-box use. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-12"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-12').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [12]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>In 2022 drop boxes were deemed unsupported by statute. But in 2020 the boxes were used under executive guidance. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-23"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-23').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [23]
          </a>
        </sup> A post-election bipartisan legislative committee later acknowledged drop boxes were unauthorized, leading to new laws.</p>
<p>An October 2021 legislative audit found no evidence of fraud via drop boxes, only that their use had no clear legal basis (Drop boxes were also video-monitored, which helped ensure integrity.) <sup class="citation">

<h6>The Use of So-called “human Drop boxes”, also not Authorized in Wisconsin Law, and Utilized in “Democracy in the Park” Efforts Coordinated by Dane County (Madison) Election Officials and the Biden Campaign</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that organized ballot collection events were illegal under Wisconsin law.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>During the 2020 elections, organized &quot;ballot collection&quot; events were held in Dane and Milwaukee counties. Voters brought their own absentee ballots to special collection sites (e.g. Milwaukee's &quot;Democracy in the Park&quot; on Sept. 29, 2020). Wisconsin law requires mail ballots be either mailed by the voter or hand-delivered to the clerk's office. These curb side events were not explicitly authorized, so they were legally ambiguous. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-13"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-13').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [13]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>Lawsuits were filed (e.g. <a href="./Democracy in the Park, Inc. v. Dane County">Democracy in the Park, Inc. v. Dane County</a>), but courts did not prohibit the events. In practice, no ballots were distributed by campaign operatives, and no court in 2020 held any collection event illegal.</p>
<p>These events prompted legislative proposals, but official audits (and the Dane County clerk's office review) found no fraudulent ballots. All counted ballots from those events were cast by the voters themselves.</p>
<h6>Allowed Election Officials to Add Missing Information to Absentee Voter or Witness Declarations, Contrary to Law, Which Says such Ballots Must not Be Counted</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Wisconsin clerks illegally added missing witness addresses to absentee ballot envelopes.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>Wisconsin law required a witness signature and address on each absentee certificate. In 2016 and again 2020, the WEC had guided clerks to cure ballets themselves by adding the missing information.</p>
<p>However, on October 3, 2022 (after the 2020 election), a Waukesha County circuit judge <a href="./Permanent Injunction">permanently enjoined</a> guidance that would allow clerks to &quot;cure&quot; missing witness addresses. The court held that a missing address meant the ballot must be rejected. In January 2024, Wisconsin's Court of Appeals confirmed that conclusion. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-14"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-14').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [14]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>The law was ultimately interpreted as barring clerks from adding missing addresses. During 2020, some clerks believed they could assist voters (following pre-election guidance), but these corrections were later deemed unlawful.</p>

          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-15"

          >
            [15]
          </a>

<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>These were voluntary guidelines, clerks did not add names to rolls without voter action. The clerks simply told voters the option existed. No evidence emerged of clerks fraudulently marking people as confined. Notably after the election the Legislature changed the rules ending automatic &quot;indefinitely confined&quot; status for many.</p>
<h5>Michigan</h5>
<h6>Mailed out Absentee Ballots to Every Registered Voter, Contrary to Statutory Requirement that Voter Apply for Absentee Ballots</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Michigan illegally mailed absentee ballots to all registered voters without applications.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>For the 2020 general election, Michigan law required voters to request an absentee ballot. The Secretary of State instead mailed applications to all 7.7 million registered voters as a convenience. No completed ballots were mailed unsolicited. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-16"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-16').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [16]
          </a>
        </sup> ^34b22a</p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>Sending applications was legally permitted and aimed at increasing turnout. (The Michigan Supreme Court, upheld broad mailing of applications to all permanent absentee voters). ^2a34ec</p>
<p>There was no court finding of illegal ballot mailings. In litigation, Republican candidates' appeals were dismissed. Official review (the independent Michigan Bureau of Elections) reported no anomalies with mailed absentee ballots. Extensive recounts and audits confirmed the outcome without irregularity. ^2cfdd9</p>
<h6>Established Remote Drop Boxes only in Heavily Democrat Precincts, without the Statutorily Mandated Video Surveillance</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Michigan strategically placed drop boxes in Democratic areas and failed to properly monitor them.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>Michigan law requires drop boxes to be distributed &quot;equitably&quot; based on population across a jurisdiction. It does not allow targeting by party. Counties set up drop boxes (e.g. 10 in Detroit) consistent with the law. Additionally, the law requires video surveillance for drop boxes installed after Oct 1, 2020. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-17"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-17').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [17]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>All drop boxes used in 2020 met state standards or were soon retrofitted with cameras. No court or audit concluded drop boxes were used improperly. Bi-partisan election officials confirmed compliance with the equitable-distribution rule.</p>
<h6>Absentee Ballots Delivered at 3am Were Counted without Affording Candidates the Opportunity to Observe, Contrary to State Law</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that Detroit illegally counted ballots in the early morning hours without proper observation.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>Michigan law explicitly allows counting of all timely ballots no matter when they arrive, provided the process follows statutory procedures. The Michigan statute permits the local canvassing board to tabulate absentee ballots &quot;in an absent voter counting board&quot; after 7 a.m. on Election Day; there is no prohibition on continuing past midnight. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-18"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-18').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [18]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>In practice, Detroit's counting continued into the early hours. Michigan law also allows challengers and watchers to remain at the counting board once counting has begun: any election challenger or attendee after processing starts must take an oath and may observe.</p>
<p>In Detroit, bipartisan observers (and the media) were present during the late-night count, and results were live-streamed. A subsequent Michigan Court of Claims judge and the Secretary of State both affirmed that late-night counting complied with law. Official recounts later verified the totals without issue. Thus, counting ballots at 3:16 a.m. or later (as often occurred) was lawful, and no statute required stopping at midnight.</p>
<h5>Arizona</h5>
<h6>Federal Court Reduced Arizona’s 29-day-before-election Registration Requirement</h6>
<p><a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a> claimed that federal courts improperly shortened Arizona's voter registration deadline.</p>
<p><strong>Background</strong></p>
<p>In late 2020, Arizona's law set the registration deadline 29 days before Election Day. Plaintiffs sued to extend it. In American Voters Alliance v. Hobbs, a preliminary injunction briefly extended the deadline to Oct 23. However, the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals partially stayed that order. On Dec. 3, 2020 the 9th Cir. allowed registrations only until Oct 15. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-19"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-claims-made-in-memorandum-two-of-the-eastman-memos-19').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [19]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>Any &quot;reduction&quot; of the deadline was by court order: the 9th Circuit ultimately trimmed the extension to just two extra days (Oct 5 to Oct 15). There was no change by legislation or executive fiat, only this limited judicial relief.</p>

            [20]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><strong>Outcome</strong></p>
<p>In federal court, Judge Andrew Gordon on Nov. 6 refused to enjoin the machine, noting ongoing state litigation. No court in 2020 found use of the machine illegal. (Indeed, the plaintiffs in the state cases withdrew after preliminary rejection).</p>
<p>Clark County's tabulation was overseen by bipartisan boards of inspectors, and the Agilis system's output was double-checked. Clark County certified its results with bipartisan agreement, and a 2022 legislative audit found all ballots were properly counted. The certified count was also confirmed by a full canvass and by state certification processes.</p>
<p>However in 2023, the Nevada Supreme Court later held that mail ballots must be checked manually, effectively overruling the use of automated matching.</p>

                        <div class="timeline-event-title">Eastman's Legal Argument to Pence's Advisors</div>
                        <div class="timeline-event-content"><p><mjx-container class="MathJax" jax="CHTML" style="font-size: 121%;" display="true"><mjx-math display="true" style="margin-left: 0; margin-right: 0;" class=" MJX-TEX"></mjx-math></mjx-container></p>
<p>
            <div class="embed">
              <div class="embed-page">
                January 5th West Wing Meeting - Eastman's Legal Argument to Pence's Advisors
              </div>
              <div class="embed-content">
                <p><mjx-container class="MathJax" jax="CHTML" style="font-size: 121%;" display="true"><mjx-math display="true" style="margin-left: 0; margin-right: 0;" class=" MJX-TEX"></mjx-math></mjx-container></p>
<p>The January 5th West Wing meeting took place between attorney <a href="./John Eastman">John Eastman</a>, <a href="./Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="./Mike Pence">Mike Pence</a>’s <a href="./Legal Counsel">legal counsel</a> <a href="./Greg Jacob">Greg Jacob</a>, and <a href="./Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="./Mike Pence">Pence</a>’s <a href="./Chief of Staff">Chief of Staff</a> <a href="./Marc Short">Marc Short</a>. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-1"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-1').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [1]
          </a>
        </sup> The purpose of the meeting was to continue the legal discussion from <a href="./January 4th Oval Office Meeting - Eastman's First Pitch to Pence">the day prior</a> and to press Pence’s advisors on the theory that the Vice President could alter or delay the certification of the 2020 election results that <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> had outlined in his <a href="./Eastman Memos">memos</a>.</p>
<p>At this meeting, <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> took the more aggressive of the two takes he outlined in his <a href="./Eastman Memos">memos</a>, that being that <a href="./Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="./Mike Pence">Pence</a> reject electoral votes from contested states outright, <a href="./Greg Jacob">Greg Jacob</a> recalled that Eastman &quot;came in and said that the request that he was there to make of us is that we reject the electors.&quot; <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-2"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-2').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [2]
          </a>
        </sup> <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-3"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-3').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [3]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p><a href="./Greg Jacob">Jacob</a> forcefully pushed back, asserting that the <a href="./Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> had <strong>no constitutional authority</strong> to unilaterally decide the outcome of a presidential election. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-4"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-4').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [4]
          </a>
        </sup> After some back and forth with <a href="./Greg Jacob">Jacob</a>, <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> acknowledged that his theory had <strong>no precedent</strong> and that the <a href="./Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> would likely rule against it unanimously. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-5"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-5').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [5]
          </a>
        </sup> Nonetheless, Eastman continued to argue that the <a href="./Electoral Count Act">Electoral Count Act</a> was unconstitutional and that the <a href="./Twelfth Amendment">Twelfth Amendment</a> could be interpreted to give the <a href="./Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> discretion in how to count votes. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-6"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-6').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [6]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p>Additionally, <a href="./Greg Jacob">Jacob</a> challenged <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> on the precedent, first asking whether he was suggesting that <a href="./Al Gore">Al Gore</a> could have declared himself the winner of the <a href="./2000 Election">2000 election</a>., <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> eventually conceded that &quot;no, there wasn't enough evidence for that&quot;. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-7"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-7').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [7]
          </a>
        </sup> By the end of <a href="./Greg Jacob">Jacob</a>'s hypotheticals, <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> conceded that &quot;no reasonable person would actually want that clause read that way&quot; acknowledging it would effectively allow one party to win perpetually. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-10"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-10').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [10]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
<p>As the conversation drew to a close, <a href="./Greg Jacob">Jacob</a> asked, &quot;Can’t we just acknowledge that this is a really bad idea?&quot; <a href="./John Eastman">Eastman</a> did not fully agree, but replied, &quot;I get everything you’re saying&quot;. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-8"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-8').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [8]
          </a>
        </sup> He concluded by stating, &quot;They’re going to be really disappointed&quot;, and then left. <sup class="citation">
          <a
            href="#embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-9"
            onclick="document.getElementById('embed-january-5th-west-wing-meeting---eastman's-legal-argument-to-pence's-advisors-9').scrollIntoView(); return false;"
          >
            [9]
          </a>
        </sup></p>
